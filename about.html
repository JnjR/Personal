INFLECTION POINT

Although the precise dates and manner of transmission are the subject of long debate, we know the ancient Greeks received the Phoenician abjad, augmented it with vowels and then diffused it further through trade and conquest.

When Plato wrote his discipline-forming dialogues, the greek alphabet was recently standardized. So much so in fact that younger contemporary Dicaearchus, a respected student of Aristotle, was able to make a special charge: by committing his thoughts to writing Plato led countless to philosophy but also caused them to philosophize superficially. This could not be said of Socrates, just two generations prior, who did not commit anything to writing. Plato gave voice to his teacher's position in the Phaedrus where Socrates retells, "the old Egyptian tale of Theuth [Thoth], the inventor of writing, showing his invention to the god Thamus, who told him that he would only spoil men's memories and take away their understandings" (274b-279b).

We are at a similar crossroad. Web-enabled technologies are gateways to legacy. Just like the genius Plato needed to master writing to have impact - a tool for which his teacher had no use - tomorrow's genius must master digital tech.

It is an exciting and scary time when the critical factors to success are things our teachers can't teach us.

This is where Bruce Lee's advice on self-help comes in. Universities are doing the next generation of philosophers a disservice by treating computers as typewriters.

There is a virtual explosion of online (often free) resources to develop your tech skills and support your continuous learning goals. Some of my favourites are Coursera, Udacity, and tuts+. If you have iTunes you can access many free classess on iTunesU as well.

... Happy New Year!

As I think about the new year to come, I am dreaming of change. Assume that the world can change, three related questions remain: 1. Why change the world? 2. How can it be changed? and, 3. What to change it into, exactly? In her book Moral Clarity, Susan Neiman puts philosophy to work on these issues.

When you pursue, “the point behind the point, the ground beneath the ground” (182), and, inspired by Spinoza, you do the hard work of exposing, “contradiction without apology” (183), you will know for yourself what Tom Paine knew, namely, that “we have it in our power to begin the world over again” (38).

Let’s mark a beginning: “Whatever else you believe about the world, only one thing is crucial: it wasn't you who made it” (232). Actually, two things are crucial: you didn't make the world and it is not – in every way – as it ought to be.

This matters because, “the gap between is and ought is the space where questions begin” (192). Therefore, the motivation for changing the world is to close the gap.

So how can change be brought about? Recognize that philosophy “circumscribes life” (25). Specifically, “our only chance to break the shackles of present reality is to begin paying attention to it” (147). So change happens against the background of what is understood to be possible.

In a society where mass media leave us feeling that a time-limited sale is more urgent than an ongoing genocide (97), we must ask more of ourselves. Our freedom makes change possible and our dignity makes it necessary.

What to change the world into, exactly? This is a tough one. The very freedom that makes change possible and even necessary also ensures that change makers will run in many different directions, in mutually incompatible directions. How can progress happen this way?

Grown up Idealists know that things are complicated. Whatever progress is made, it will not be about more fun. My favourite statement of the book: “If our reason for living was the pleasure we get from it, the species would be doomed” (159).
